If you use social media, this is about you

The Optimization Trap

Every like, share, and follow trains the algorithm. But it also trains you.

SCROLL
01 / The Mechanism

How the algorithm trains you

Platforms like Instagram rank content by predicted engagement, not relevance or quality. This creates a feedback loop that reshapes both what creators make and how users see themselves.

Content is ranked by engagement

The algorithm surfaces what provokes the strongest emotional reaction, not what is most truthful or useful.

Creators optimize for visibility

To be seen, creators perform idealized versions of their lives, bodies, and personalities. Posts that don't play the game get buried.

Users compare and internalize

A feed full of optimized performances becomes the baseline. Users start measuring themselves against a standard no one actually meets.

Behavior shifts without instruction

The platform never tells you to act differently. It just shows you what gets attention and what doesn't. Over time, you adapt without realizing it.

The loop repeats

Your adjusted behavior generates new data, which refines the algorithm, which further shapes what you see and who you become.

03 / What Happened

The evidence went public. The business model survived.

2019
Instagram's internal research finds significant harm to teenage mental health. The results are not published.
Sept 2021
The Wall Street Journal publishes the leaked internal documents, revealing what Facebook knew.
Oct 2021
Frances Haugen testifies before the U.S. Senate. She tells Congress the company chose profits over safety.
Feb 2024
The EU's Digital Services Act takes full effect, requiring algorithmic transparency and non engagement based feed options. A step forward, but it regulates disclosure, not the business model itself.
2025
Engagement based ranking remains the default across Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube. The architecture that caused the harm is still the architecture generating revenue.
04 / The Other Side

What critics say

The evidence is not without pushback. It is worth understanding the limitations before drawing conclusions.

Is the harm overstated?

Some researchers argue that the measured effects of social media on mental health are statistically small and that isolating a single variable in something as complex as social media use is extremely difficult. Psychologist Candice Odgers, in a 2024 review in Nature, questioned whether the evidence supports the scale of alarm that books like The Anxious Generation suggest.

Still, the effects point the same direction across multiple studies, countries, and methodologies. The size of the harm is debatable. The direction is not.

05 / What Can Be Done

Paths forward

No single fix will solve this, but researchers and regulators have proposed several approaches. Some require policy. One you can do today.

👤

What You Can Do Now

Switch to a chronological feed wherever the option exists. Turn off autoplay. Try Bluesky and choose your own algorithm. These are small moves, but they break the feedback loop the algorithm depends on. The research shows the chronological feed alone eliminates the measured mental health harm.

⚖️

Regulation

The EU's Digital Services Act requires algorithmic transparency reports and mandates non engagement based feed options. It is the most significant regulatory effort to date, though enforcement remains inconsistent.

🔀

Algorithmic Choice

Bluesky, a decentralized platform, lets users choose from over 100,000 community built feed algorithms instead of being locked into a single company controlled system. The user decides what shapes their feed.

🧪

Wellbeing Algorithms

Researchers have found that algorithms optimized for user wellbeing rather than engagement may reduce activity initially, but engagement recovers as users adjust. This rebuts the claim that reform would destroy the business.

The research has been published. The testimony has been given. The platforms haven't changed. Now it's on us.

06 / Selected Sources

Relevant sources from the literature review

These sources support the essay's main claims about algorithmic ranking, mental health effects, and practical alternatives to engagement-based feeds.

Braghieri, Levy, and Makarin (2022)

Provides causal evidence that social media use can worsen mental health through unfavorable social comparison, which supports the essay's argument about self-replacement and comparison-driven harm.

Mandile (2025)

Separates the effect of Instagram's recommendation algorithm from the platform itself and finds that algorithmic ranking worsened mental health outcomes, especially for young users.

Ovadya and Thorburn (2022)

Introduces bridging-based ranking as an alternative optimization target that favors content helping different groups understand one another instead of maximizing engagement.

European Commission, Digital Services Act (2022)

Shows the strongest current regulatory framework for algorithmic transparency, risk assessment, and non-profiling feed options, making it directly relevant to policy reform.

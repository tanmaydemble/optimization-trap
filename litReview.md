Literature Review: Annotated Sources
In light of the detrimental effects of algorithms powering short-form content,
what can users and tech companies change to improve or combat the current state of affairs?
Thesis Statement:
Social media platforms use engagement-based recommendation algorithms that reward an idealized presentation and penalize authenticity, creating a cycle of self-replacement that harms users' mental health. While individual awareness and whistleblowing have failed to change the system, emerging alternatives such as bridging-based ranking, innovative algorithm design, decentralized platform architectures, and regulatory frameworks like the EU Digital Services Act offer practical meaningful alternatives that reduce algorithmic harm without requiring users to stop using social media apps.

Instagram does not rely on a single algorithm. Instead, it uses several ranking systems, each tailored to a different feature of the app: Feed, Reels, Stories, and Explore. In January 2025, Instagram head Adam Mosseri confirmed that the three most important ranking signals are watch time, likes per reach, and sends per reach (DM shares) [1]. As of 2026, Instagram different ranking metrics under "Views" as its primary metric, and about 94% of content comes from AI recommendations rather than chronological ordering. A December 2025 update also introduced "Your Algorithm," a feature letting users reset or adjust their recommendations, while penalizing reposted or aggregated content in favor of original work.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The ranking elements mentioned above originate straight from Adam Mosseri, the head of Instagram, who has consistently conveyed algorithm improvements via official Instagram channels. The Sprout Social guide consolidates these announcements with platform statistics and has been widely used by industry professionals. Although these sources lack peer review, they serve as primary documentation of the system's real operation. In a literature analysis focused on the algorithm's mechanics, official platform remarks represent the most authoritative source, as Instagram does not disclose its ranking code.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source introduces the technical basis for the whole review. Because watch time and DM shares are the most important ranking signals, material needs to be designed to keep people's attention in the first three seconds and feel shareable, not necessarily to be real or significant. Instagram seems to understand that users want more control over their feeds, as seen by the addition of "Your Algorithm" options. However, these controls are only optional changes on top of the same system that is designed to get the most engagement.

"The Facebook Files" was a series that The Wall Street Journal released in 2021. It was based on inside business records that former employee Frances Haugen leaked. Another study indicated that 17% of teen females stated Instagram made their eating disorders worse. Even though these results were clear, the company's management did not adjust the design of the main product to fix the problems that had been found.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The "Facebook Files" series was based on real internal documents that were given to both the press and the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. The Wall Street Journal is one of the most regarded investigative news outlets in the United States. The information comes from Facebook/Meta's own researchers, which makes it unique because it represents the company's own opinion of its own product. The series won a George Polk Award for its reporting. The research wasn't peer-reviewed in the usual academic way, but it was done by trained researchers at the corporation and shown to the top management.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source gives evidence that algorithmic damage is not accidental. The essay talks about how the platform shows idealized content because it gets people interested, and users change how they act in response. The released research verifies that this process yields quantifiable effects, especially for young women. It is more crucial for the thesis that the corporation knew about these problems and decided not to change the product. This gap between what people know and what they do is what makes the problem structural rather than just a lack of information or attention.

Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
In October 2021, Frances Haugen, a former Facebook product manager who worked on algorithmic product management, gave testimony to the U.S. Senate. She said, "Facebook's products hurt kids, create division, and make our democracy weaker" [3]. Haugen said that the platform's ranking methodology based on engagement was to blame for these problems. This is because content that is hateful, controversial, or inflammatory tends to get more engagement, which means the algorithm boosts it even more. She said that Congress should set rules for algorithmic ranking and that platforms should stop using engagement as their main goal for optimization. Haugen had already given the SEC and Congress tens of thousands of internal documents. Even while there was a lot of media coverage and testimony, no substantial federal laws were passed in response, and the basic algorithmic design of Facebook and Instagram stayed the same.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
Haugen's testimony is very important because she worked with algorithmic systems at several big internet companies, such as Google, Pinterest, and Yelp, before joining Facebook. She gave regulators and journalists hundreds of internal papers that backed up her accusations. Federal whistleblower laws shield her for telling the SEC and Congress about what she knew. MIT Technology Review is a respected magazine that focuses on technology and its effects on society. It provided the analysis that is quoted here. The biggest problem is that Haugen's point of view is that of one employee, and Facebook disagreed with some of her descriptions.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
Haugen's testimony is important not only for what it said but also for what didn't happen after it. There was no federal law passed. The algorithm was not changed. This substantiates the concept that individual actions, including thoroughly documented whistleblowing, cannot supersede the structural incentives inherent in engagement-based platforms. It also changes the focus of the policy discourse from content control to algorithmic design.
Source #4
Full citation:
L. Braghieri, R. Levy, and A. Makarin, "Social media and mental health," American Economic Review, vol. 112, no. 11, pp. 3660-3693, Nov. 2022. doi: 10.1257/aer.20211218.
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
This study provides causal evidence linking social media use to worsening mental health among college students. The researchers used a natural experiment: the staggered introduction of Facebook across U.S. college campuses between 2004 and 2006. By combining data on the specific dates each college gained access to Facebook with survey data from the National College Health Assessment, the authors applied a generalized difference-in-differences strategy. They found that the rollout of Facebook at a given college increased symptoms of poor mental health, particularly depression [4]. Students who were predicted to be most susceptible to mental illness showed increased use of mental healthcare services after Facebook became available. The study also found that students were more likely to report that poor mental health was impairing their academic performance. The mechanism the authors identified was unfavorable social comparison.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
American Economic Review is one of the best economics journals in the world. Articles published there go through a strict peer review process. The study's quasi-experimental design mitigates the primary limitation of most social media research, which is its dependence on correlations instead of causal inference. The authors utilized the staggered implementation of Facebook across institutions to compare mental health results prior to and subsequent to the platform's introduction. 
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
Most research on social media and mental health is correlational, which leaves the direction of causation unclear: do unhappy people use more social media, or does social media make people unhappy? This study provides evidence for the latter. The mechanism it identifies, unfavorable social comparison, maps directly onto what the essay describes: the algorithm surfaces idealized content, users compare themselves to it, feel inadequate, and begin adjusting their self-presentation. This is the academic foundation for the "self-replacement" dynamic at the center of the thesis. The study is also notable for showing effects even before recommendation algorithms existed on Facebook, which suggests that social comparison is a feature of the platform itself, not just of algorithmic amplification.
Source #5
Full citation:
J. Haidt, The Anxious Generation: How the Great Rewiring of Childhood Is Causing an Epidemic of Mental Illness. New York, NY, USA: Penguin Press, 2024. See also: C. L. Odgers, "The great rewiring: is social media really behind an epidemic of teenage mental illness?" Nature, vol. 628, pp. 29-30, Mar. 2024. doi: 10.1038/d41586-024-00902-2.
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
Jonathan Haidt argues that the shift from a "play-based childhood" to a "phone-based childhood," concentrated in the early 2010s, has caused an epidemic of anxiety, depression, self-harm, and suicide among adolescents. He documents increases of 134% in anxiety and 106% in depression between 2010 and 2018, with Gen Z hit hardest [5]. He proposes four reforms: no smartphones before high school, no social media before age 16, phone-free schools, and more unsupervised outdoor play. However, developmental psychologist Candice Odgers published a review in Nature challenging Haidt's causal claims. She argues that meta-analyses across 72 countries show no consistent association between well-being and social media rollout, and that the evidence for screen time as a direct cause of mental illness is "equivocal" [5]. She suggests that the correlation may partly reflect reverse causation or may distract from deeper structural causes of youth distress.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
Haidt is a social psychologist at New York University and the author of several influential books on moral psychology. The Anxious Generation spent over 52 weeks on the New York Times bestseller list and shaped significant public discourse and policy proposals. However, Haidt is not a clinical psychologist or media researcher, and some of his evidence relies on trend graphs that illustrate correlation rather than causation. Odgers is a developmental psychologist at the University of California, Irvine, who has studied adolescent mental health and digital technology for 20 years. Her review was published in Nature, one of the most prestigious scientific journals. Including both perspectives provides a balanced view of a genuinely contested scientific question.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
Haidt's data on rising anxiety and depression since the early 2010s provides the broader context for the thesis, while Odgers' challenges Haidt’s claims. My thesis is not that social media causes depression in a general sense. It is that engagement-based algorithms create a cycle where users perform optimized versions of themselves because the platform rewards performance over authenticity. The review positions itself between Haidt’s and Odgers’ perspectives by focusing on a mechanism neither fully examines: the algorithm's role in conditioning users to become curated versions of themselves.Source #6
Full citation:
S. Mandile, "The dark side of social media: Recommender algorithms and mental health," CESifo Working Paper No. 11648, Jan. 2025. [Online]. Available: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5130959
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
This paper isolates the effect of Instagram's recommendation algorithm on mental health, separate from the effect of using the platform itself. Before March 2016, Instagram displayed posts in reverse chronological order from accounts a user followed. In March 2016, Instagram introduced an algorithmic feed that ranked content based on predicted engagement. Mandile uses a quasi-experimental design, combining Dutch longitudinal data with a difference-in-differences strategy, to examine what happened to users' mental health after the algorithm was introduced [6]. The study finds that the introduction of the algorithm worsened mental health outcomes, with the effect most pronounced among young adults aged 16 to 20. The timing of the decline in mental health aligns with the deployment of the algorithm rather than with the mere existence of the platform.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
CESifo is a well-regarded European economic research network based in Munich, and its working papers are widely circulated in academic economics. While this paper has not yet undergone formal journal peer review (it is a working paper as of January 2025), the methodology is rigorous: difference-in-differences estimation is a standard and well-understood causal inference technique. The use of Dutch longitudinal data provides a large sample and repeated observations over time. The paper was presented at the CESifo Area Conference on Economics of Digitization in 2024, where it received feedback from established researchers in the field.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source is critical because it separates two things that most research conflates: the effects of social media as a platform and the effects of the recommendation algorithm specifically. Before 2016, Instagram users saw content from people they chose to follow, in the order it was posted. After 2016, an algorithm decided what they saw, optimized for engagement. Mandile's finding suggests that it is not simply being on Instagram that harms mental health; it is the algorithm's curation of content that does it. This distinction is central to the review's research question. If the algorithm is the source of harm, then redesigning the algorithm, rather than abandoning the platform entirely, becomes a viable path forward.
Source #7
Full citation:
H. Metzler and D. Garcia, "Social drivers and algorithmic mechanisms on digital media," Perspectives on Psychological Science, vol. 19, no. 5, pp. 735-748, Sep. 2024. doi: 10.1177/17456916231185057.
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
This paper provides a framework for understanding how algorithms interact with pre-existing social dynamics on digital media. The authors argue that the public debate too often conflates time spent using social media with algorithmic effects, when in reality algorithms build on social drivers like status-seeking, social comparison, and group identity [7]. The paper describes a feedback loop: users seek attention and recognition, they reproduce behaviors that the algorithm rewards, and the algorithm adapts to reinforce those behaviors. Metzler and Garcia note that even the earliest version of Facebook (2004 to 2006), which had no recommendation algorithm, still produced negative mental health effects. They propose several design interventions, including hiding engagement metrics for certain types of posts, gradually exposing users to perspectives that differ from their own, and optimizing recommendation systems for metrics other than engagement, such as consensus or belief accuracy.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
Perspectives on Psychological Science is a high-impact, peer-reviewed journal published by the Association for Psychological Science. The authors are researchers at the Complexity Science Hub in Vienna, and the paper synthesizes a broad range of empirical studies and theoretical frameworks. Its strength is that it does not advocate for a single position but instead maps the landscape of evidence and identifies research gaps. 
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source directly addresses the feedback loop the thesis describes: the cycle where the platform rewards certain performances, users adapt to earn those rewards, and the algorithm reinforces the adaptation. It also introduces a crucial nuance. Algorithms amplify harmful social dynamics, but they did not create them. Social comparison existed before Instagram. This means that even a perfectly designed algorithm would not eliminate the problem entirely; it would just stop making it worse. The design interventions proposed here, such as hiding like counts or optimizing for consensus rather than engagement, give the review concrete alternatives to discuss rather than simply calling for vague reform.

A. Ovadya and L. Thorburn, "Bridging-based ranking," Belfer Center for Science and International Affairs, Harvard Kennedy School, Tech. Rep., May 2022. [Online]. Available: https://www.belfercenter.org/publication/bridging-based-ranking. See also: L. Thorburn and A. Available: https://theconversation.com/how-to-redesign-social-media-algorithms-to-bridge-divides-216321
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
Bridging-based ranking is an alternative algorithmic design that promotes content receiving positive engagement from people who normally disagree with each other. Where engagement-based ranking tends to amplify divisive content because divisive material generates more clicks and reactions, bridging-based ranking creates incentives for content that fosters mutual understanding across different groups [8]. The concept has already been implemented in limited forms. Twitter/X's Community Notes (formerly Birdwatch) uses a variant of this approach. Polis, an open-source civic technology platform, has been used in Taiwan to surface perspectives that bridge divides in public policy discussions. Early evidence from these implementations suggests that bridging-based ranking can reduce partisan animosity and improve the quality of online interactions. However, the authors acknowledge open questions: which divides should be bridged, whether bridging might amplify mainstream views at the expense of minority perspectives, and whether the goals of bridging and engagement are fundamentally in tension.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The Belfer Center at Harvard Kennedy School is one of the leading public policy research centers in the United States, with a strong focus on technology governance. Aviv Ovadya is a researcher specializing in information ecosystem integrity, and Luke Thorburn is a researcher at King's College London working on algorithmic design. The report has been cited in policy discussions by the EU, the Knight First Amendment Institute, and multiple academic papers on recommender system design. The 2025 article in The Conversation provides an updated, accessible summary of the ongoing research. The main limitation is that bridging-based ranking has not yet been tested at the scale of a major platform's main feed.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source offers the most developed alternative to engagement-based ranking currently available. Bridging-based ranking goes further than transparency: it is a different optimization target entirely. Rather than asking "what will keep this user scrolling?" it asks "what will build understanding across groups?" The fact that it has been tested in real systems (Community Notes, Polis) moves it from theory to practice. Full citation:
J. Lasser et al., "Designing social media content recommendation algorithms for societal good," Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, May 2025. doi: 10.1111/nyas.15359.
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
This paper proposes a framework for designing content recommendation algorithms that serve the public good rather than maximizing engagement. It surveys the emerging field of alternative recommendation algorithms, including bridging-based ranking, intelligence-based ranking, and chronological feeds, and evaluates each against the standards set by the EU's Digital Services Act [9]. One important finding is that alternative algorithms that optimize for well-being, consensus, or information quality rather than engagement may initially reduce user engagement, but engagement rebounds over time as users adjust to the new environment. The paper also addresses the tension between algorithmic intervention and freedom of expression, arguing that the design of recommendation algorithms is itself a value-laden decision that should be made through democratic processes involving multiple stakeholders, not by platform companies alone.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences is a peer-reviewed journal published by one of the oldest scientific organizations in the United States. The authors represent multiple European research institutions and bring interdisciplinary expertise in computational social science, media studies, and policy. The main limitation is that many of the alternative algorithms discussed are still in early stages of development and testing.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
This source provides the academic framework for asking the review's central question: can the current algorithm be replaced with something better? It surveys the full landscape of proposed alternatives rather than advocating for a single solution, which makes it useful for a literature review. The finding that engagement may recover after an initial dip is important because it undermines the primary objection tech companies raise against algorithmic reform: that changing the algorithm would destroy the business. If users eventually engage just as much with a healthier feed, then the argument that profit and well-being are inherently opposed loses its force.
Source #10
Full citation:
European Commission, "The Digital Services Act," 2022, fully applicable Feb. 2024. [Online]. Available: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-services-act. See also: Freedom House, "The EU Digital Services Act: A win for transparency," 2024. [Online]. Available: https://freedomhouse.org/article/eu-digital-services-act-win-transparency. See also: AlgorithmWatch, "A guide to the Digital Services Act," 2025. [Online]. Available: https://algorithmwatch.org/en/dsa-explained/
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
The Digital Services Act (DSA) is the most significant regulatory intervention targeting social media algorithms enacted to date. Fully applicable since February 2024, it imposes specific obligations on Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs), defined as those with more than 45 million monthly users in the EU. These obligations include publishing transparency reports on content moderation and algorithmic systems, conducting risk assessments for systemic harms, providing vetted researchers with access to platform data, banning targeted advertising to children, prohibiting dark patterns, and offering users the option to receive content through a non-profiling-based recommendation system such as a chronological feed [10]. As of October 2025, Article 40.4 granted researchers the right to request non-public data from VLOPs. Enforcement actions have included a 45 million euro fine against X for a non-compliant advertising repository. However, early assessments suggest that the DSA has not yet fundamentally altered the engagement-based business model; transparency reports can be structured to favor the platform's interests, risk assessments remain largely inaccessible to the public, and the default user experience on most platforms is still shaped by engagement-optimized algorithms.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The DSA is binding EU law, making the European Commission's official documentation a primary legal source. Freedom House is a well-established nonpartisan organization that evaluates democratic governance and civil liberties worldwide. AlgorithmWatch is a nonprofit research and advocacy organization based in Berlin that specializes in algorithmic accountability and has been closely involved in DSA implementation monitoring. Together, these sources provide the legal text, a civil liberties perspective, and a technical accountability perspective on the same regulation. The main limitation is that the DSA is still in its early enforcement phase, and its long-term effects remain uncertain.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
The essay mentions the GDPR as an example of regulation that produced "cookie consent popups that people ignore" rather than structural change. The DSA is a more targeted attempt, specifically addressing algorithmic transparency and systemic risk. For the review, it represents the most ambitious regulatory framework currently in force. The requirement that platforms offer non-algorithmic feed options is directly relevant to the research question, as it creates a legal mandate for something like chronological ordering as an alternative. However, the early evidence of the DSA's limitations supports a measured conclusion: regulation can create transparency and accountability, but it has not yet changed the default experience for most users, which is still shaped by engagement-based algorithms.
Source #11
Full citation:
Bluesky Social PBC, "Algorithmic choice," Bluesky Blog, Mar. 30, 2023. [Online]. Available: https://bsky.social/about/blog/3-30-2023-algorithmic-choice. See also: J. Graber et al., "Bluesky and the AT Protocol: Usable decentralized social media," in Proc. ACM CoNEXT-2024 Workshop on the Decentralization of the Internet, 2024. doi: 10.1145/3694809.3700740.
Summarize the source using formal language. Use at least one in-text citation:
Bluesky is a social media platform built on the AT Protocol, an open and decentralized communication protocol. Its core innovation is what it calls a "marketplace of algorithms": rather than locking users into a single company-controlled ranking system, Bluesky allows users to choose from over 100,000 community-built feed algorithms, or to create their own [11]. The platform separates content hosting (Personal Data Servers), content indexing (Relays), and content presentation (App Views), so that no single entity controls what users see. Moderation is similarly decoupled: users can subscribe to independent labeling and moderation services. As of September 2025, Bluesky had roughly 1.5 million daily active users. The 2024 ACM workshop paper describes the technical architecture and explains how the system's openness allows anyone to contribute to content moderation and community management. However, critics have noted that giving users algorithmic choice does not address the structural features of short-form public posting itself. The combination of brevity, anonymity, low barriers to entry, and instant publishing may produce toxic dynamics regardless of the ranking algorithm.
Evaluate the credibility of the source:
The Bluesky blog post is a primary source from the company itself, describing its own design philosophy and architecture. The ACM workshop paper is a peer-reviewed academic publication presented at a conference organized by the Association for Computing Machinery, one of the leading professional organizations in computer science. Jay Graber, the CEO of Bluesky and lead author of the workshop paper, has been a prominent figure in the decentralized technology community. The main limitation is that Bluesky is still a small platform relative to Instagram or TikTok, and it is not yet clear whether its model can scale to hundreds of millions of users while maintaining its decentralized principles.
Discuss how this source applies to your topic/thesis:
Bluesky represents the most prominent real-world attempt to answer the research question from the platform-architecture side. It does not just tweak the algorithm; it asks who should control the algorithm. This is philosophically aligned with the essay's concern about agency: if the problem is that users have no say in how their attention is directed, then giving them that say is a meaningful intervention. But the critique raised by observers is equally important: if the basic structure of short-form social media encourages harmful dynamics like social comparison and performance anxiety, then even perfect algorithmic choice will not solve the underlying problem.
